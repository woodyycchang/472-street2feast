Beijing, the capital of the People's Republic of China, is a city where ancient history and modern development meet. It is home to some of the country's most famous landmarks, including the Forbidden City, the Temple of Heaven, and a section of the Great Wall. Beijing's culinary scene is equally impressive, with Peking Duck being its most renowned dish.

Peking Duck

Beijing’s most famous dish—roasted duck with super-crispy skin and soft pancakes.

Chefs in Beijing built this dish over many generations. It started in royal kitchens and later spread to the city’s restaurants. The key is the shiny, crisp skin, sliced thin, then wrapped with scallion and sweet bean sauce. It became a “must-eat” for visitors and a proud symbol of the capital.

Fried Sauce Noodles

Thick wheat noodles topped with rich soybean-paste sauce and diced pork.

Locals call it zhajiangmian. The dish grew out of North China and Shandong cooking, then became a Beijing favorite. In the capital, cooks fry yellow soybean paste with a little sweet wheat paste, add lots of scallion and diced pork, and serve it over thick handmade noodles with crunchy vegetables. Beijing’s version ended up the best-known style across China.

Instant-boiled Mutton

Paper-thin lamb swished in hot broth, then dipped in sesame sauce.

People often link this hotpot to the Mongol era, but records show the quick-slice, quick-boil method existed even earlier. In the Qing dynasty it became a popular Beijing dish. The tall copper chimney pot and sesame dipping sauce are now classic signs of a Beijing hotpot house.

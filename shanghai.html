Shanghai, a vibrant city on China's central coast, is the country's biggest city and a global financial hub. Its heart is the Bund, a famed waterfront promenade lined with colonial-era buildings. Across the Huangpu River rises the futuristic skyline of the Pudong district. Shanghai cuisine, known for its sweetness, includes famous dishes like xiaolongbao (soup dumplings), braised pork belly, and steamed perch.

Soup Dumplings

Delicate wrappers filled with rich broth and pork, eaten hot with vinegar and ginger.

These dumplings took shape in Nanxiang on Shanghai’s outskirts in the late Qing era. Cooks set meat stock to gel, wrapped it with seasoned pork, and steamed the bundle so the jelly melted back into broth. Sold around Nanxiang Old Street, the style spread citywide and became a Shanghai icon.

Braised Pork Belly

Glossy, sweet-savory cubes of pork belly braised with soy, rock sugar, and wine.

Around the Jiangnan region, chefs perfected a “red-braise” that uses soy sauce, rock sugar, and Shaoxing wine to create a deep mahogany glaze. Shanghai’s take is the gentlest—silky fat, balanced sweetness, and a sheen that became the city’s signature comfort style.

Steamed Perch

A light steam with ginger and scallion that lets the fish shine.

Steaming is a Jiangnan hallmark prized for purity of flavor. In Shanghai, river and estuary fish are seasoned lightly, steamed just through, and finished with hot oil over ginger and scallion—an old-school restaurant ritual that showcases freshness over heavy seasoning.
